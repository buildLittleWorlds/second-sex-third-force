The Second Sex and the Third Force

*Includes Neo-Freudians, Post-Freudians, the Organismic and Gestalt schools, Lewinians, Phenomenologists, the Self, Growth and Human- istic schools, etc., etc., etc.

Little girls are much less apt to be disturbed emotionally than are little boys who are their agemates. Child guidance clinics report seeing four boys to every girl. Far fewer girls have speech problems and reading problems, fewer drop out of school before graduation, and fewer become delinquent--all in contrast to male counterparts of the same age. These girls seem to come near to fitting definitions of creative productivity and well-functioning mental health--they are effective person- alities. They combine, as do creative artists and scientists at the adult level, the feminine qualities of humane concern, aesthetic sensibility and good communication skills with the masculine traits of efficiency, theoretical orientation, intel- lectual aggressiveness, and ego-strength.

Studies of high school students¬π and college students¬≤ show girls rating higher than boys on qualities and attitudes related to creativity and mental health. Girls not only have creative attitudes but are also productive and contributive mem- bers of the school society. They consistently get better grades than boys, both in high school and college, they hold more of- fices, and participate in more activities. There are occasional complaints about the girls being less spirited and more self critical than the boys, but in general they produce and do it well and happily.

In contrast to the positive mental health and creative attitudes of the school girls, adult women¬≥ are said to be more neurotic than adult men.‚Å¥ They often retreat into private and family worlds and no longer receive the rich stimulation of the outer worlds--the technological, aesthetic, natural, and human. They do not continue to enhance their old knowledges and to re- arrange what they know to meet the emerging requirements of an ever-changing world. To put it simply, such women can scarcely carry on interesting conversations with their husbands. The problem is--if the truth were known--that they did not find real commitments, genuine interests, true foci for lifetime learning in school. As students they did well what was expected and had their basic needs met--they were accepted, recognized, and suc- cessful. However, they did not become self-actualizing and autonomous‚Åµ and therefore were unable to work out a meaningful, productive life in relation to both the family and the larger world. Outside reinforcements, pleasing others (the teacher) remained necessary for them for they had not gone beyond the need motivation level. After the young woman supplants being a student with being a homemaker, the expectancies as to tasks and interests are no longer intellectually challenging and so- ciety does not apparently, except in stereotypic terms, value the tasks required of the mother and the housewife. The woman who does not already have a commitment to an intellectual venture, and ego strength as well, may feel confined by the restrictive role that society prescribes. In such a situation, some women regress to an intellectually inactive state and others realize something is wrong but find few patterns and little encourage- ment for changing this state of affairs.‚Å∂

The fact that the psychological and intellectual needs of adult women seemingly are not being met is accompanied by societal problems of a grave and pressing nature. The paramount problem is, of course, survival. There seems to be little doubt where everyone stands on this and with an ever-increasing humane- ness and kind-heartedness we are willing to say that all should survive.‚Å∑ Beyond survival (which might turn out to be a sub- sistence-level affair--I understand they are raising algae in sewage but that people are having trouble digesting the chloro- phyl.) we can turn to other societal concerns--for example, im- proving the human condition.

The human condition can be described in many dimensions, all of which show deficiencies in development. It is readily apparent that physical man in the world at large is still a hun- gry, ill and generally unfortunate creature. The average man in India lives only half as long as the average Englishman. The South American lives to be 35--North Americans live to be 70, and as C. Wright Mills‚Å∑·µÉ said, perhaps this is why we are re- sented so much. The thinking man (which we assume an adequate world citizen must be) remains a Hellenistic ideal still to be realized. Ignorance and prejudice prevail. Over half of the adults in the world cannot read and write and more than half of the children are not in school.

World problems are approached, and sometimes even equalled, in poignancy and urgency by the problems that have developed or that we have always had with us in the United States. Health and mental health in the positive sense are just being defined and the old problems--from pestilence to dementia--are by no means solved. The shortage of nurses, the refusal of many hos- pitals to accept Negroes, the prevalence of disease among Amer- ican Indians, and the absence of any concern for the health of migrant workers--all sully our dreams of American citizens as men of dignity.

Nor have educational needs, despite our claims to liter- acy, been met adequately. Many of our schools--we can point a finger at the south and elsewhere--are a national disgrace. Schools can and do create environmental deprivation which, in turn, produces perceptual defect‚Å∏ and an inadequacy in learning. We have done too little to provide meaningful materials and ex- citing activities for the one-third who come to school not ready to learn to read;‚Åπ and we have similarly failed those who never learned quite well enough--who started out behind and lost ground every year thereafter--the slow learners, the disabled readers, the potential drop-outs, and the pre-delinquents.

We know very little about how young children learn and still less about adolescent learning (although we have documented their dating and delinquency patterns). The research has not been done and the theory has too often ignored and too rarely focussed upon the actual teaching situation.¬π‚Å∞ Fortunately there are the beginnings of some scholarly investigation and well-designed experimentation in the schools, but there is also a crucial need for inventive programs and innovative teachers. What was right for grandpa is not right for the child of today and tomorrow, and I have never been fully convinced that it was right for grandpa.

These are only suggestions of the human needs our society faces. I have spelled out educational needs in greater detail since my own research is in this area and I am more acutely aware of deficiencies. However, mental health problems should be men- tioned--such as the fact that 4 out of 5 counties have mental health services and that it is estimated that 7-12 per cent of American school children are emotionally disturbed.

This paper, presenting as it does an overview of the individual woman's and the collective society's problems, would be patently inadequate if it did not allude to the societal prob- lems of women who work. After all, a recognition of these prob- lems resulted in the establishment of the Commission on the Status of Women of which this committee on education is a part. The economic and social inequalities that women face are well known by at least the members and affiliates of the commission. And surely we would also agree that there are too many Americans who have unmet needs and too many people in the world who suffer from excessive want and privation. (Since we are not yet communi- cating with other planets we do not know whether or not this is par for worlds at large.) It takes only a superficial grasp of the situation to realize that the attitudes and talents women have--such as aesthetic sensitivities, communication skills, and concern for human beings--could go far in meeting some of these problems.

Of course, recognizing problems and even know- ing an answer is not enough to insure action (knowing the con- sequences, i.e., throat cancer, doesn't keep us from smoking). The obvious use of such able and relatively unemployed women on real and urgent problems may be difficult due to known prej- udices and unexplored intolerances. Yet somehow we must bring the woman with her needs and the society with its needs into a complementary and mutually enhancing relationship.

These concerns with human problems are relatively recent. The mental health movement stems from Clifford Beers' pioneer work in the early 1900's, and programs of special education and intensive work with the physically afflicted have largely devel- oped since World War II. As C. P. Snow¬π¬π has commented, society is at last showing a trend toward human kindness. A group of adolescents recently discussed this point and one of the brash- est but most idealistic said spontaneously, "It's cool to care." And the psychologist Shoben¬π¬≤ has said with obvious relief, "At last it's respectable to be responsible."

Not only can we document such observations but we also can cite two parallel trends in theory that seem to bode well for man's future development. There is a developing literature on what is called the positive evolution of society as well as much that has been written on the positive evolution of man. Surely as humane concerns become increasingly respectable and positive development (not just curing of illness and remedying privation) is more generally valued and fostered, women will be encouraged to work in this direction. Our hope is that they can become fully developed (with the peripheral benefit of being personally pleased with the product) as well as socially contrib- utive.

A large number of disciplines are studying and develop- ing portrayals of the evolved individual. He is variously de- scribed in psychological terms as mature, self-actualized, fully- functioning, and productive.¬π¬≥ A more social-psychological view, embracing such concepts as humaneness, "universal man," and emerg- ing mind, has been expressed by anthropologists and biologists.¬π‚Å¥ Historians and philosophers speak of heroes and philosopher-kings.¬π‚Åµ

Beyond the philosophical and theoretical outlines--the sketched-in model of what man may be, should be, and occasionally has been--there are at present a number of studies (completed or in process) of creative-effective individuals.¬π‚Å∂ These in- dividuals, as we have come to know them through the descriptions, continue to grow and change and develop--to enhance what they are, to become more nearly what they might be. They are described as planful and optimistic and outwardly concerned. Many of them have extended self beyond the microcosm of their own concerns into the macrocosm of societal need. In fact, the most mature focus primarily on problems outside of themselves. They have social conscience, feel for and with mankind, and want to con- tribute to the public welfare.

The idealized society and the idealized image of the individual bear striking similarities, especially in such an area as humane concern and human development. The good society¬π‚Å∑ as it has been conceptualized would seem to foster the develop- ment not only of thoughtful and far-seeing individuals but also to allow far more people to develop their potentialities. It is my assumption¬π‚Å∏ that certain values--kindness and tenderness as well as creative innovation (growth)--are better than other val- ues--hostility and destruction and lack of growth (stagnation or regression).

It is now felt that qualities of the good society and of the effective and humane individual¬π‚Åπ can be abstracted and that some of the antecedents (early stages of growth) of this effec- tive adulthood can be scientifically documented.¬≤‚Å∞ A question arises as to whether these qualities, denoted as creative and mentally healthy in the adult, can be found in children or ado- lescents. The companion question is: if creative qualities can be found in certain of our youth, do they give us an adequate basis for predicting creative and effective behavior in adult- hood? From exploratory studies recently done it is our belief that these qualities are apparent in the young adolescent, and we assume from our studies of the biographies and case histories of creative adults that the early emergence of such qualities is one of the best indicators we have of creative-effective adult development. We have yet to learn how to foster and facilitate the growth of these qualities; however, several theories have been developed and a good deal of educational experimentation is being directed toward this end.

Psychological research (particularly in certain studies of learning as in learning to read) has shown that young people who are positively disposed will respond better than those who are negatively disposed toward the proffered development. For example, good readers--students who like to read--characteris- tically improve more when given special reading help than do poor readers--students who do not like to read (although the poor readers might seem to have more room for development).

In our research we have been able to identify three rather distinct value systems or achievement drives in bright young people. We have tentatively called these types creative intellectuals, studious, and social leaders and have (through the study of their behavior and attitudes by observations, inter- views, questionnaires, and attitude, personality and value scales) found certain rather clearly marked differences that indicate that the creative intellectuals are developing quite differently than the other groups of adolescents. From the evidence that we have so far, we believe this to be in the direction that has been described as self-actualizing.

These three adolescent growth patterns¬≤¬π can be placed on a continuum to show differences more clearly. The creative intellectual is placed on the end that seems to us to be more predictive of development of a humanistic and idealistic system of values, the studious falls somewhere near the center and characteristically tries to maintain traditional values, and the social leader falls at the other end of the scale and favors hedonistic values. Our preliminary research on these types has consisted of personality, attitude, and value assessment of almost 1000 adolescents of superior ability and a comparison group of several hundred average students. Observations and individual interviews have supplemented this information. Of these students about 20 per cent of the intellectually superior (almost equally divided between boys and girls) and 5 to 10 per cent of the average may be said to have a creative-intellectual orientation.

The creative intellectuals are concerned about the enhance- ment and improvement of the state of man and his affairs. They believe in growth, planned change, creative innovation, and are generally optimistic. They not only feel that individual man and social institutions can be improved, but many of them also seem willing to work on the projects. In contrast the studious (60 per cent of our superior students, with an over-balance of girls) seem to be mainly concerned with following the dictates of parents and teachers. They do not rebel or ask for progress or change but instead want to conserve or preserve. They are fine, upright citizens--getting exceptionally good school grades and the best citizenship ratings from their teachers--and have both Protestant ethic (in Max Weber's terms) and "conventional wisdom" (as John Galbraith has described it).

The social leaders march to quite a different drummer. They espouse what is sometimes called the dominant American value system--hedonism and materialism. This group (20 per cent of the superior adolescents with an over-balance of boys) is interested in personal gain (affluence, power) and do not seem to worry about the destruction or exploitation that might be necessary to accomplish this. They express disbelief in man's ability to im- prove, and feel that institutions need not change. They will probably be church members if such actions prove to be socially advantageous, but they have little belief in or tolerance for religious and philosophical thought or for formulations that depict man as basically good.¬≤¬≤ They see man as a conniver and an exploiter and feel that they must master the game.

Thus with tendencies toward destruction-exploitation (often in the name of pleasure and personal profit) on one end of the continuum and a belief in enhancement-improvement on the other end, the issue seems clear. These creative intellectuals are the young people who we predict might become creative-effective adults with humane concerns. Their behavior and belief patterns are much like those of the creative, self-actualizing adults now being studied. There are, it has been noted, as many girls as boys in this group. The girls seem to be characteristically feminine with somewhat more of the subjective-asthetic orientation in contrast to the boy's objective-theoretical orientation; but they seem to be developing a good balance in terms of male-female qualities. The adolescent girl seems to have much in the way of potentiality. The question is will she become self-realized as this term is now being defined, i.e., will she fully develop and fully use her abilities?

The evidence seems to indicate that women, even most of those who are intellectually able and creatively inclined, have not developed fully nor made social contributions in line with their promise and potential. Male researchers report that women contrasted with men have contributed fewer new theories, been less influential in social change, and developed fewer recognized artistic innovations.

As was noted earlier, we have been working with the test results of a large number of able adolescents over the past few years and assessing them in terms of creative-productive dimensions. Much of our data has been gathered by using the same instruments that McConnell and Heist¬≤¬≥ have used in their studies of able college students. Interestingly enough, despite allegations that adult women are not particularly creative in terms of such accomplishments as musical compositions and scientific discoveries, girls generally test higher than boys in attitudes that are desig- nated as creative, e. g., complexity, aesthetism, thinking intro- version, and social maturity. Our studies also show girls as being relatively more open (a quality needed for creative develop- ment) and less prejudiced (a quality of obvious importance in human concern for other people.) Boys, even very able ones, receive higher scores (and thus are measured as less tolerant) on the Rokeach Dogmatism and Rigidity Scales.

Beyond the promise shown in terms of creative and open attitudes, we may also look at the potentialities of the able female in other ways. There is little evidence that she is intellectually less capable than her male counterpart although she may be somewhat more highly developed in verbal areas and relatively less developed (or skillful) in mathematical and scientific areas. This special female verbal facility is apparent very early: little girls learn to talk sooner and the story goes that they talk more (and certainly with less difficulty).¬≤‚Å¥ They also learn to read more easily and seem to maintain an advantage in at least communication mechanics and techniques (skill in reading, spelling--fluency in handwriting).

Women are also socially sensitive and humanely concerned. Among girls these characteristics can be seen as preferences for people over things (they do not readily make creative innovations when faced with a red fire truck but they do very well on the nurse's kit, as Torrance points out). Although girls are less aggressive socially than boys and often talk less in mixed groups, they tend to be sensitive to individual differences and to be considerate and accepting of others. As Torrance says, "In our culture, sensitivity is a feminine virtue, while independence is a masculine virtue."¬≤‚Åµ However, for the girl or woman to be creative she will need to be independent as well as sensitive.

Further, women seem to be particularly sensitive in an aesthetic sense. With the adult woman this aesthetic sensitivity is often at the appreciator or at the do-it-for-my-own-enjoyment level and not in terms of a real drive to stimulate interest in art for all through education or to paint or sculpt in transcendant ways. There seems to be reason to believe that here is an area in which women have real strength in terms of inclin- ations and an area in which our society (with ever-increasing leisure time and the goal of complete development for each indivi- dual) has great needs. This aesthetic realm, at least in terms of large scale involvement of people, is practically uncharted.

We have seen that there are bases for describing individuals as having different value systems, kinds and intensities of interests, styles of thinking, openness to possibilities, potential for commitment, humane concerns, aesthetic sensitivities, verbal skill, possibility (liking for) involvement in intellectual tasks, desire to make a contribution and to make changes, and belief in self and human beings in general. We have seen that there are as many girls as boys who seem to have what we have termed creative-intellectual predispositions and humanitarian- idealistic value systems. We also feel that despite all these abilities and inclinations women do not develop fully in a personal sense nor contribute fully in a societal sense. The crucial issue is how to cultivate these creative-contributive women-in-embryo to increase growth in the directions specified.¬≤‚Å∂

It is my contention that there are good risks and bad risks in any enterprise. Researchers often comment that certain investigators almost always get results because they design their studies in ways that pay off. This thinking may relate to discussions of serendipity and luck. Many scientists say that we make our luck by being well prepared and ready with the facts when a lucky accident happens.¬≤‚Å∑

Interpreted in terms of helping girls to develop fully and make social contributions, I would say that this means that some girls are good bets and others are not. All women suffer when a girl is given a scholarship and does not finish school or when a woman gets a medical degree and never practices or when a woman walks out on a job in the middle of the year without prior warning because she wants to marry or move with her husband. I feel that we now have a few guidelines for choosing girls who have strong creative-contributive inclinations and who may persevere in their commitments. We are beginning to be able to pick out the good risks. It is my feeling that the good risks will be most apt to make full use of scholarships and encouragement. By helping the girls who appear to have commitment and concern for society and a strong desire to transcend themselves, we may find more women making important contributions. It is here that education might make a difference.

Out of the literature concerned with mentally healthy and creative people and the few things we know and/or surmise about creative women,¬≤‚Å∏ we find that one of the aspects of cre- ativity is a desire--even a deep-rooted need--to go beyond the self. Gordon Allport speaks of this as an extension of the self. This going beyond "the self" and beyond the "here and now" and beyond "everyday reality" to make of life more than it appears to be is all important.¬≤‚Åπ If we add to this transcendental per- spective and vision a belief in a cause of a new solution or a new art form, we have what I have termed commitment.

It is my thesis that for women commitment may be more important than career. The engagement with problems can be car- ried on in all of the discontinuities of a woman's life.¬≥‚Å∞ With a goal and a dedication a woman can move from student role to researcher to professional practice to part-time consultant work to full-time homemaking. With genuine commitment she can work on the problem that she feels most important in any of these contexts. The point is that the posing of questions and the searching for answers must always be there--not only maintained but enhanced. (She writes *Gone with the Wind* on grocery sacks and tries out new mathematical formulas on a blackboard above her sink.)

Beyond the fact that a need for commitment is essential for a creative and a contributive life, there is also the need for learning how to learn. Not only is there the fact that one must seek and continue search all through life, but that the boundaries and avenues of search must be defined.¬≥¬π How do we develop ways of creating or inquiring or solving problems or thinking critically?

Recently researchers have been experimenting with spe- cific approaches that are felt to foster creative thinking,¬≥¬≤ inquiry method,¬≥¬≥ discovery,¬≥‚Å¥ Socratic questioning,¬≥‚Åµ and other problem solving approaches.¬≥‚Å∂ In discussing these new approaches and also the new curricula Cronbach has this to say, "The aim of the new curricula is less to equip the student with the know- ledge he is likely to need than to advance the student's awareness of the origin of knowledge, its trustworthiness and its limita- tions. More important than his learning any one fact in school is his willingness to learn from the discipline during his adult life."¬≥‚Å∑ All of these methods of "learning how to learn" and the new ways of looking at the structure of knowledge have ad- vantages over rote-learning and recitation¬≥‚Å∏ approaches in that they give the learner more autonomy, an essential when the learner is on his own as the woman may be during parts of her life, and certainly an essential for anyone who hopes to be creative.

The development of commitment and of critical thinking skills, then, are the aims I am suggesting for creative-intel- lectual girls. The goals (as is usually the case in education) can be more readily set than the processes by which we attain them. However, there are clues emerging from educational and psychological research as to the kind of educational experiences-- methods and approaches--that are most likely to develop such qualities.

Self-awareness (What are my needs, values, talents?) and an awareness of possibilities (What are the arrays of social causes, educational reforms, new art forms, or scientific break- through?) are vital before a choice of direction or a commitment can be made. Self-awareness and clarification of goals can be fostered by counseling (particularly client-centered counseling approaches) and awareness of possibilities can come about by a wide variety as well as challenging and stimulating educational exposures.

Models can do much to supply awareness of both what to do and how to do it, what the world offers and how to deal with the offerings. Since creative-effective female models who also have social charm and feminine appeal (seemingly a necessity if we are to lure the young) are not always available,¬≥‚Åπ there must be carefully planned programs for presenting such models. Biographies and fiction, radio, tapes, film and personal appear- ances all may be used. These should be prototypic women (ideal- ized images) who face the full and complex world (family responsi- bilities included), have developed a dedication in terms of work- ing on a significant problem, and are more shapers-of than shaped- by their environment.

The pattern is indispensable but students (especially girls) also need encouragement and recognition. Someone who says, "You would make a fine scientist" or "I see that you have a special flare for writing" and follows this up with specific suggestions and help when it is needed. The encourager-recog- nizer-promoter-patron-sponsor (male or female) differs from the model (although the person may be the same) in that this indi- vidual (the sponsor) is more than a pattern-of-life to be identi- fied with and studied. The sponsor must, in addition, take the active role of recognizing and encouraging talent and predisposi- tion in the girl. A teacher or a family friend may play this role although often it is enacted by a parent or a relative.

Beyond direct-line human contacts there should be oppor- tunities for growth supplied in the school, home or community-- laboratories, discussion groups, art and music classes, cultural events, books and magazines. (Girls who do not sample the vari- ety of rich offerings in our middle-sized and large cities are losing much of their education.) And as the girl begins to mature there must be chances to participate actively--to sponsor or direct discussions, to be a laboratory assistant, to lead a group in social innovation, to perform in musical events, or to display her paintings. There are no limitations, in terms of geograph- ical locale, to the possibilities for participation--‚Å¥‚Å∞ and the doing is unbelievably rewarding. As one girl said, "The girls who haven't organized or *done* something on their own just don't know what they are missing!"

With all this commitment and these lasting skills, the question now arises: what are women to do? Probably many of the answers come from their own predispositions and talents. This does not mean that with the great need for talented tech- nologists women should necessarily eschew these areas. (Women make excellent computer programmers and fine laboratory tech- nicians.) However, since the great needs of our society are for maintenance and enhancement of the natural and aesthetic worlds and for far-reaching innovations in the human world, and since our creative-concerned girl has been shown to have communi- cation skills, humanitarian leanings and aesthetic awareness, there is every reason to encourage these girls to develop in ways that their talents and attitudes lead them.

Again there must be awareness. In the presentation of the world and its complexities, there should be a companion pre- view of the problems to be solved. Creative and intelligent young people are characteristically more interested in what is not known, than what is known--what is not solved, than what is a *fait accompli*. They want something to work on and towards, and if they are mentally healthy and have adequate ego-strength they do not object to the task being difficult and complex.

In the broadest terms, the problems that should be pre- sented to our able girls and particularly to the creative intel- lectuals include the ones that were discussed earlier--survival, the human condition, basic needs (educational, mental health and physical health) and enhancement possibilities (such as prob- lems in positive mental health and in the aesthetic world).

Bibliography: Education for Women

A Bibliography of What Has Been Said*
1950 - 1961
Evelyn Perloff
Division of Educational Reference
Purdue University

The present bibliography is the first draft of a listing and categorizing of references in the area of education for women, which appeared in U.S. publications during the eleven year period, 1950-1961. It's major purpose is to include all that has been said (1950-1961) on the one specific sub-topic of the woman problem, their education. References covering other areas of the woman problem (employment, status, health, etc.) are also to be presented in separate bibliographies, with cross-references included. A first addition, however, is to be a 1962 bibliography on the education of women.

The following indexes were the primary sources utilized in compiling this bibliography on women's education: (1) Books in Print, (2) Education Index, (3) Guide to Guidance, (4) Psychological Abstracts, (5) Readers' Guide, and (6) Sociological Abstracts. Only those references which either dealt directly with the education of women or were obviously related to specific aspects of educating women were included. Two other limiting factors were: (1) specificity to the United States, and (2) use of the English language. No attempt was made to evaluate the references critically. Pertinency was the crucial determinant. Of all the potentially pertinent references, 284 were finally selected. These references were divided into two mejor sections (with several subsections): I. Education of Women - General References, and II. Education of Women - Higher Education. The present subject-matter groupings are not perfect. They contain errors of omission as well as commission. There are most likely incorrect and/or overlapping placements, inappropriate references, unimportant selectons, and failures to include pertinent articles. A great many but unfortunately not all references were skimmed. It is planned that the final draft of each biblio-~ graphy will result from a review of every reference, and will, therefore, be annotated.

*Gratitude is expressed to Dr. Hermann H. Remmers, Director, Division of Educational Reference, for his continuing encouragement and support of the project.

Supplement 1 (1961-1962)
