THE SECOND SEX AND THE THIRD FORCE Little girls are much less apt to be disturbed emotionally than are little boys who are their agemates.

Child guidance clinics report seeing four boys to every girl.

Far fewer girls have speech problems and reading problems, fewer drop out of school before graduation, and fewer become delinquent--all in contrast to male counterparts of the same age.

These girls seem to come near to fitting definitions of creative productivity and wellfunctioning mental health--they are effective person- alities.

They combine, as do creative artists and scientists at the adult level, the feminine qualities of humane concern, aesthetic sensibility and good communication skills with the masculine traits of efficiency, theoretical orientation, intel- lectual aggressiveness, and egostrength.

Studies of high school students~ and college students show girls rating higher than boys on qualities and attitudes related to creativity and mental health.

Girls not only have creative attitudes but are also productive and contributive mem- bers of the school society.

They consistently get better grades than boys, both in high school and college, they hold more of- fices, and participate in more activities.

There are occasional complaints about the girls being less spirited and more self critical than the boys, but in general they produce and do it well and happily.

They do not continue to enhance their 01d knowledges and to re- arrange what they know to meet the emerging requirements of an everchanging world.

To put it simply, such women can scarcely carry on interesting conversations with their husbands.

The problem is--if the truth were known--that they did not find real commitments, genuine interests, true foci for lifetime learning in school.

As students they did well what was expected and had their basic needs met--they were accepted, recognized, and suc- cessful.

However, they did not become selfactualizing and autonomous‚Äù and therefore were unable to work out a meaningful, productive life in relation to both the family and the larger world.

The woman who does not already have a commitment to an intellectual venture, and ego strength as well, may feel confined by the restrictive role that society prescribes.

Beyond survival (which might turn out to be a sub- sistencelevel affair--I understand they are raising algae in sewage but that people are having trouble digesting the chloro- phyl.) we can turn to other societal concerns--for example, im- proving the human condition.

The human condition can be described in many dimensions, all of which show deficiencies in development.

It is readily apparent that physical man in the world at large is still a hun- gry, ill and generally unfortunate creature.

The average man in India lives only half as long as the average Englishmen.

The thinking man (which we assume an adequate world citizen must be) remains a Hellenistic ideal still to be realized.

Over half of the adults in the world cannot read and write and more than half of the children are not in school.

World problems are approached, and sometimes even equalled, in poignancy and urgency by the problems that have developed or that we have always had with us in the United States.

Health and mental health in the positive sense are just being defined and the old problems--from pestilence to dementia--are by no means solved.

The shortage of nurses, the refusal of many hos- pitals to accept Negroes, the prevalence of disease among Amer- ican Indians, and the absence of any concern for the health of migrant workers--all sully our dreams of American citizens as men of dignity.

Nor have educational needs, despite our claims to liter- acy, been met adequately.

Many of our schools--we can point a finger at the south and elsewhere-~are a national disgrace.

Schools can and do create environmental deprivation which, in turn, produces perceptual defect¬Æ and an inadequacy in learning.

We have done too little to provide meaningful materials and ex- citing activities for the onethird who come to school not ready to learn to read;~ and we have similarly failed those who never learned quite well enough--who started out behind and lost ground every year thereafter--the slow learners, the disabled readers, the potential dropouts, and the predelinquents.

We know very little about how young children learn and still less about adolescent learning (although we have documented their dating and delinquency patterns).

The research has not been done and the theory has too often ignored and too rarely focussed upon the actual teaching situation.

Fortunately there are the beginnings of some scholarly investigation and welldesigned experimentation in the schools, but there is also a crucial need for inventive programs and innovative teachers.

What was right for grandpa is not right for the child of today and tomorrow, and I have never been fully convinced that it was right for grandpa.

These are only suggestions of the human needs our society faces.

I have spelled out educational needs in greater detail since my own research is in this area and I am more acutely aware of deficiencies.

However, mental health problems should be men- tioned--such as the fact that 4 out of 5 counties have mental health services and that it is estimated that 7-12 per cent of American school children are emotionally disturbed.

This paper, presenting as it does an overview of the individual woman's and the collective society's problems, would be patently inadequate if it did not allude to the societal prob- lems of women who work.
